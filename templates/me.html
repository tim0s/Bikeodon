Performance Management Chart
What does this show?

The PMC models how your body adapts to training load over time using Training Stress Score (TSS) as the currency of effort.

  • Fitness (CTL) — Chronic Training Load. A 42-day exponential weighted average of daily TSS. Think of it as your aerobic base: slow to build, slow to lose.
  • Fatigue (ATL) — Acute Training Load. A 7-day EWA. Spikes quickly after a hard block and falls fast during recovery.
  • Form (TSB) — Training Stress Balance = CTL − ATL. Positive = fresh and ready to race. Deeply negative = accumulated fatigue. A "hot spot" for peak performance is roughly +5 to +25 after a taper.

The two-component model was first described by Banister (1991) and is the basis for Morton et al. (1990). Coggan's TSS formulation is the de-facto standard in cycling; see TrainingPeaks: NP, IF, TSS.

Note: TSS is only computed for activities with power data and a set FTP. Activities without TSS contribute 0 to the chart. Set your FTP in Settings → Charts.

About training zones
Heart rate zones

HR zones define exercise intensity relative to your maximum heart rate. The percentages shown are the upper boundary of each zone. Zone 2 (endurance) riding is the foundation of aerobic fitness; most polarized training plans recommend 80% of training volume there. Zone 5 (VO₂ max) work is high-intensity and should make up a small fraction of total training.

The five-zone model used here follows the widely adopted system described in Seiler & Kjerland (2006) and the British Cycling / TrainingPeaks conventions. Your max HR can be estimated as 220 − age, but a field test gives a more accurate value.

Power zones

Power zones are set relative to your Functional Threshold Power (FTP): the highest average power you can sustain for approximately 60 minutes. The seven-zone Coggan model is the de-facto standard in cycling.

FTP can be estimated from a 20-minute all-out effort (take 95% of the average power) or a dedicated ramp test. See Coggan's training levels for the full model.

Mean maximal power curve
What is the power curve?

The Mean Maximal Power (MMP) curve plots the best average power you have ever produced for each duration — from a 5-second sprint to a 60-minute effort. It is a comprehensive fingerprint of your physiological capabilities.

  • 5s / 30s — neuromuscular / anaerobic capacity
  • 1 min / 5 min — VO₂ max power range
  • 20 min — a proxy for FTP (95% of 20-min best ≈ FTP)
  • 60 min — true functional threshold power

The model traces back to Monod & Scherrer's critical power concept (1965) and was popularized in cycling by Coggan. See Pettitt (2014) for a modern review.
